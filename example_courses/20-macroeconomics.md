## Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP is the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a specific time period.

### Approaches to GDP
1. **Expenditure Approach**: $\text{GDP} = C + I + G + (X - M)$
   - $C$: Consumption
   - $I$: Investment
   - $G$: Government Spending
   - $X - M$: Net Exports
2. **Income Approach**: Sum of all incomes (wages, rents, interest, profits).
3. **Value-Added Approach**: Sum of value added at each stage of production.

## Real vs Nominal GDP

- **Nominal GDP**: Measured in current prices. It can increase simply because prices rose, even if production stayed the same.
- **Real GDP**: Adjusted for inflation. It reflects the actual volume of production.

## What is Inflation?

Inflation is a general increase in prices and a fall in the purchasing value of money.

## Types of Inflation
- **Demand-Pull Inflation**: Occurs when demand for goods exceeds supply ("too much money chasing too few goods").
- **Cost-Push Inflation**: Occurs when production costs (like wages or oil prices) increase, forcing companies to raise prices.
- **Built-in Inflation**: Result of the price-wage spiral.

## Measuring Inflation: CPI

The **Consumer Price Index (CPI)** tracks the price of a fixed basket of goods and services.
$\text{Inflation Rate} = \frac{CPI_{\text{new}} - CPI_{\text{old}}}{CPI_{\text{old}}} \times 100$

## The Role of the Central Bank

Central banks (like the Fed or ECB) use monetary policy to achieve price stability and full employment.

## Tools of Monetary Policy
- **Interest Rates**: Raising rates makes borrowing expensive, slowing down inflation.
- **Open Market Operations**: Buying/selling government bonds to control liquidity.
- **Reserve Requirements**: Setting how much cash banks must keep in their vaults.

## Expansionary vs Contractionary Policy
- **Expansionary**: Lowers rates to stimulate growth.
- **Contractionary**: Raises rates to cool down an overheating economy.
